# Post Activation Potentiation

## Definition
A phenomenon where performing a maximal or near-maximal contraction temporarily enhances subsequent performance. Two distinct mechanisms are involved: PAP and PAPE.

## PAP vs PAPE

### PAP (Post Activation Potentiation)
- **Mechanism**: Myosin filaments moving closer to actin, enabling more rapid cross-bridge formation
- **Duration**: 1-5 minute effect
- **Effect**: Produces instantaneous force increase across the entire force-velocity spectrum

### PAPE (Post Activation Performance Enhancement)
- **Mechanism**: Unknown — persists even in the presence of supraspinal CNS fatigue, ruling out CNS involvement; can be triggered in seconds, ruling out muscle temperature
- **Duration**: Up to ~15 minutes
- **Status**: Described as "very mysterious" — insufficient data to determine the mechanism

## Practical Application

### How to Create a PAP Effect
- Requires **one single maximal-effort repetition** — no more
- Can be done with any load, as long as effort is maximal
- No need for multiple sets or complex warm-up pyramids
- The single rep triggers the physiological effect without creating fatigue

### Application to Warm-ups
- Traditional warm-up pyramids (multiple sets progressing to working weight) may not be optimal
- A single maximal-effort rep after general warm-up may be sufficient for PAP
- This is particularly relevant for machine-based exercises where technique is less demanding
