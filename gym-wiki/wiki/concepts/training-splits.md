# Training Splits

## Definition
Training splits are program structures that divide exercises across multiple workout sessions, typically organized by muscle groups, movement patterns, or body regions. Common splits include upper/lower, push/pull/legs, torso/limbs, and body part splits.

## Physiological Basis

### Supraspinal CNS Fatigue Effect
The primary limitation of training splits is supraspinal central nervous system fatigue:
- Post-workout fatigue mechanisms include calcium ion accumulation, excitation-contraction coupling failure, and muscle fiber damage
- Calpains (proteases) are released in response to calcium accumulation, causing damage over 4-6 hours post-workout
- Inflammatory response peaks ~6-8 hours post-workout and can last 24-36 hours
- Inflammatory molecules enter bloodstream and create sensation in the brain
- This causes global fatigue effects - training one muscle impairs performance in ALL muscles

### Recovery Timeline Impact
- Full body workouts: Fatigue washes out by ~36 hours, allowing 3x/week training
- Upper/lower splits: Supraspinal fatigue from day 1 impairs day 2 performance
- 5-6 set splits: May push recovery to 48+ hours
- Post-workout fatigue is a "package deal" - high volume creates all mechanisms proportionally

## Common Split Types

### Full Body (ABA or AAA)
- Trains all muscle groups each session
- 3x/week frequency per muscle (optimal for hypertrophy)
- Allows maximum training density with minimal fatigue interference
- Volume limited to 1-3 sets per muscle for recovery

### Upper/Lower Split
- AB pattern: Upper day, lower day, rest, repeat
- Back-to-back training days create supraspinal fatigue interference
- Day 2 recruitment limited by day 1's inflammatory response
- Only beneficial for single-set protocols or reduced volume approaches

### Push/Pull/Legs
- Typically 6x/week frequency (PP/L/PPL cycle)
- Successive days create cumulative supraspinal fatigue
- Requires significantly reduced volume per session

### Bro Split (Body Part Split)
- Once per week frequency per muscle
- Requires 4-5+ sets per muscle to achieve growth
- High volume per session, no fatigue carryover between sessions
- Slowest progress due to low frequency

## Evidence-Based Recommendations

### Frequency vs Volume Tradeoff
- Higher frequency splits require lower volume per session for recovery
- Lower frequency splits require higher volume per session
- 3x/week full body with 1-2 sets per muscle often superior to 1x/week high volume

### Progressive Overload Considerations
- Full body allows easier monitoring of progression (same exercises, same days)
- Split routines complicate progression tracking due to different exercises/muscle focus
- AB splits particularly good for comparing AB day performance directly

### Practical Applications
- Full body AAA or ABA splits are physiologically optimal baseline
- Upper/lower every other day (upper/off/lower/off) minimizes fatigue issues
- Any split with consecutive training days needs volume management
- Single sets in splits may be insufficient for advanced trainees
