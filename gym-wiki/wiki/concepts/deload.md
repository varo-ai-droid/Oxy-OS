# Deload

## Definition
A planned period of reduced training volume or intensity designed to allow accumulated post-workout fatigue to dissipate. Deloads are necessary when training volume exceeds recoverable levels over an extended period.

## When Deloads Are Needed

### Training Hard vs Training Silly
- **Training hard**: High effort (central motor command) with low volume — does NOT require deloads
- **Training silly**: Excessive volume (4+ sets per exercise, high volumes, long eccentrics, lengthened partials) — DOES require deloads
- If you're doing single sets or recoverable volumes (e.g., 3 sets 3x/week), you likely never need a planned deload

### Signs You Need a Deload
- Accumulated fatigue from excessive volume over multiple weeks
- Plateau in strength or performance despite adequate effort
- Persistent soreness or lack of recovery between sessions
- After completing a high-volume specialization block

## How to Structure a Deload

### Option 1: Reduced Volume (Mild Fatigue)
- Drop back to a 3x/week full body AB split or full body A
- Single set per exercise, 3 times per week
- Maintains some training stimulus while allowing recovery

### Option 2: Reduced Frequency (Moderate Fatigue)
- Drop to 2x/week training
- Single set per exercise
- Longer recovery windows between sessions

### Duration
- **Mild accumulation**: 1 week of reduced volume
- **Moderate accumulation**: 2 weeks
- **Severe accumulation**: Up to 4 weeks (rare, typically from extreme volumes)

### Fatigue Debt Calculation
If you exceed recoverable volume, each excessive workout adds to a "fatigue debt":
- 3 workouts/week with 1 extra day of unrecovered fatigue = 3 days/week of accumulating debt
- After 4 weeks: ~12 days of accumulated fatigue needing recovery
- This is why deloads or reduced volume phases are necessary after high-volume training blocks

## Common Misconceptions

### "You need a deload every 4-8 weeks"
- Only true if you're accumulating fatigue during that period
- If volume is recoverable, no deload is needed
- Planned deloads imply you're writing a program that accumulates fatigue (the actual problem)

### "Deload means complete rest"
- Complete rest is rarely necessary
- Reduced volume training maintains some stimulus while allowing recovery
- Maintenance-level volume (1-2 sets per muscle, 2-3x/week) is sufficient to prevent atrophy
