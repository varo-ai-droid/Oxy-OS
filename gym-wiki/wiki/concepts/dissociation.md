# Dissociation

## Definition
A training principle where secondary muscle groups in compound movements receive insufficient stimulation to grow because motor unit recruitment is focused on the primary (limiting factor) muscle, leaving secondary muscles in a partial/recruited but non-stimulating state.

## Physiological Basis
When performing multi-joint exercises, the brain recruits motor units to match the force demands of the movement. However, due to voluntary activation limitations and the concept of "limiting factors," only one muscle reaches maximum recruitment while secondary muscles remain sub-maximally recruited - neither truly resting nor receiving full stimulation.

This creates a "dissociated" state where secondary muscles are neither:
- Fully relaxed (recruited without meaningful load)
- Fully stimulated (reaching stimulating rep thresholds)

## Practical Implications
- Secondary muscles in compound movements do NOT receive "half sets" worth of stimulus
- Either get full stimulating reps or minimal benefit - no proportional middle ground
- Training status affects dissociation: Beginners recruit multiple muscles for growth; Advanced lifters typically only stimulate one muscle per exercise

## Evidence
- Multi-joint exercises with multiple high-force muscles still show single-muscle hypertrophy patterns
- Untrained individuals show multi-muscle growth from single exercises
- Trained individuals typically show growth in only the limiting factor muscle
