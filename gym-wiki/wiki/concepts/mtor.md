# mTOR

## Definition
The mammalian target of rapamycin (mTOR) is a kinase enzyme that regulates cell growth, including muscle protein synthesis. It acts as a central hub integrating mechanical, nutritional, and growth factor signals to control protein translation.

## Role in Hypertrophy

### Mechanical Tension Signaling
- High mechanical tension activates mechanosensitive pathways (titin, focal adhesion kinase)
- These pathways feed into mTOR signaling to initiate protein synthesis
- Heavy loads produce stronger mTOR activation than light loads

### Eccentric Contractions
- Enhanced mTOR signaling compared to concentric-only contractions
- Eccentric loading produces greater mechanical tension per unit of metabolic cost
- This may partially explain why eccentric-focused training produces robust hypertrophy

### Stretch-Mediated Signaling
- Stretch-under-load activates pathways that can be independent of mTOR
- Titin protein senses muscle elongation and triggers signaling
- Can add sarcomeres in series (longer muscle fibers) through mTOR-independent pathways

## Relation to Training Frequency

### The Tagayaki Studies
- Rodent studies using electrically stimulated contractions examined mTOR activation timing
- Found that mTOR signaling peaks and returns to baseline within a specific window post-contraction
- The recovery/repair process (muscle damage inflammation) is separate from mTOR-driven hypertrophy signaling
- **Key insight**: mTOR activation returning to baseline does NOT mean the muscle cannot be stimulated again

### mTOR vs Recovery
- Post-workout MPS elevation (~150% increase in first 6-12 hours) is driven by mTOR
- MPS returns to baseline by ~36 hours post-workout
- The repair of muscle damage (inflammatory process) is a separate, non-mTOR process
- Training frequency is NOT limited by mTOR signaling duration — it's limited by recovery from fatigue/damage

## Practical Implications
- mTOR activation from mechanical tension is the primary driver of hypertrophy
- Training before mTOR signaling has fully subsided does NOT impair growth (the stimulus from the new workout initiates new mTOR signaling)
- Recovery capacity, not mTOR timing, determines optimal training frequency
- Nutritional factors (leucine, protein intake) support mTOR activation but don't override mechanical signaling
