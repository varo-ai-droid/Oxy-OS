# Three Ways to Increase Revenue

## Definition
The **Three Ways to Increase Revenue** framework identifies the fundamental levers every business can pull to grow. All business growth strategies fall into these three categories: **Price, Volume, and Frequency** (or value per customer).

## The Three Levers

### 1. Price
Increase revenue per transaction by charging more for your offering.

#### Tactics:
- **Raise Prices Directly** - Increase prices by 50-100% or more
- **Dynamic Pricing** - Different prices for peak vs. off-peak times
- **Strategic Framing** - Present price changes as discounts ("weekdays 20% less" vs "weekends 20% more")
- **Afford Marketing Spend** - Use higher prices to fund customer acquisition

#### Key Principle:
Most businesses are underpriced. If your price increase doesn't significantly hurt sales, you're charging too little.

#### Price Testing Questions:
- What happens if I double my price?
- Can I raise it 50% without losing more than 10% of customers?
- What price would make this feel like a "steal" to my customer?

### 2. Volume (Customers/Sales)
Increase the number of customers or transactions.

#### Tactics:
- **Increase Marketing Efforts** - Ads, outreach, content, partnerships
- **Optimize Acquisition Channels** - Split test, improve conversion rates
- **Improve Sales Process** - Better scripts, presentations, follow-up
- **Target Decision-Makers** - Address principal-agent problem in B2B

#### Key Principle:
Most businesses either have too many customers (supply problem) or too few (demand problem). Identify which constraint you face.

#### Volume Testing Questions:
- Where are leads dropping off in my funnel?
- What's my current conversion rate at each stage?
- How can I get 2x the leads with the same resources?

### 3. Frequency / Value Per Customer
Increase the total value derived from each customer relationship.

#### Tactics:
- **Upsell/Cross-sell** - Additional services, upgrades, add-ons
- **Change Service-to-Delivery Ratio** - 1:1 → small group → 1:many
- **Leverage Existing Customer Base** - Affiliate offers, intangible products
- **Improve Team Sales Skills** - Train staff to sell more during interactions

#### Key Principle:
Customers who have already bought are in a "yes" mindset. This is your easiest audience to sell to.

#### Frequency Testing Questions:
- What would happen if I offered this during every customer interaction?
- How can I serve more customers without adding capacity?
- What related services do my customers actually need?

## Supply vs. Demand Constraint Framework

### Identifying Your Constraint

**Test Question:**
*"If you doubled your ad budget tomorrow, could you double your sales or would you just create a mess you couldn't handle?"*

#### Demand Problem (Pull the Price/Volumes Levers)
- You can handle more customers
- You'd make more money with more sales
- **Solution:** Focus on attracting more customers with better offers

#### Supply Problem (Pull the Efficiency Levers)
- You can't handle more customers
- More customers would create chaos
- **Solution:** Focus on systems, scaling, delivery optimization

### The Accordion Effect
Constraints naturally shift between supply and demand as you solve them. Successful scaling requires recognizing and adapting to the new constraint.

## Revenue Formula

```
Revenue = Price × Volume × Frequency
```

All business growth comes from increasing one or more of these factors. The key is knowing which factor is **constrained** and addressing it first.

## Application Process

### Step 1: Identify Current Constraint
- If sales are stagnant → Price problem
- If you're turning away customers → Supply problem
- If you have inventory but no buyers → Demand problem

### Step 2: Apply the Right Levers
- **Price-constrained:** Improve offer, raise prices, add value
- **Supply-constrained:** Create systems, delegate, improve processes
- **Demand-constrained:** Increase marketing, improve targeting, remove friction

### Step 3: Test and Measure
- Make one change at a time
- Give each change 30-90 days to prove itself
- Track the single most important metric
