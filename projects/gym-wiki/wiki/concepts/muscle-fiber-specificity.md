# Muscle Fiber Specificity

## Definition
Hypertrophy adaptation is specific to individual muscle fibers activated during exercise. Different exercises activate different subsets of fibers, even within the same muscle group, due to voluntary activation deficits and neuromechanical matching.

## Physiological Basis

### Voluntary Activation Deficit
- **Definition**: Inability to voluntarily recruit all available motor units in a muscle
- **Mechanism**: Limited by maximum tolerable perception of effort from brain
- **Magnitude**: Larger muscles (glutes, quads) show deficits of 20%+, smaller muscles show smaller deficits
- **Implication**: Different exercises recruit different "unshared" fiber populations

### Neuromechanical Matching with Fatigue
- **Process**: Different exercises use different recruitment pathways
- **Result**: Each exercise has unique unactivated fibers at maximum effort
- **Spectrum Effect**: Degree of overlap varies between exercises

## Programming Implications

### Exercise Variation
- **Shared Fibers**: Get adequate stimulus across multiple training sessions
- **Unshared Fibers**: Only receive stimulus when that specific exercise is performed
- **Frequency Requirement**: Each unique fiber population needs training twice weekly

### Full Body ABC Problem
- Each exercise in ABC routine trains unique fiber populations
- Those unshared fibers only get one stimulus per week
- Results in maintenance (3-4 sets once weekly) rather than growth
- Not optimal for maximizing muscular potential

### Full Body AB Solution
- Each exercise repeated twice weekly
- Both shared and unshared fibers get adequate stimulus
- Optimal for long-term hypertrophy

## Evidence
Based on interpolated twitch technique measurements showing voluntary activation deficits exist in all individuals, indicating different exercises access different fiber populations.
